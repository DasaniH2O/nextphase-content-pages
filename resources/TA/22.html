AI Persona Realism · Free Resource · 2026-07-22

An AI character stays consistent when you lock its identity once and QA every frame.

The realism checklist from the video — 18 checks in four passes. Micro‑details are what kill the “this looks AI” reaction, and every one of them is decided before you batch a single clip.

A

Identity lock

do once, before clip 1
Master reference set. Generate one canonical face in 5–8 fixed angles (front, 3/4 left, 3/4 right, profile, smile, neutral). Every future generation conditions on these — never on a previous output, or drift compounds.
Written face spec. Note the measurable stuff in plain words: eye color (hex if your tool takes it), eyebrow shape, nose bridge width, jawline, teeth (one slightly crooked beats perfect), face shape.
Two permanent marks. Pick exactly two — a mole, a freckle cluster, a faint scar — and write down their positions. Marks are your fastest drift detector later.
Fixed hair spec. Color, length, texture, and which side the part falls on. Parts silently flip between generations; the spec is what catches it.
Skin texture floor. Visible pores, fine vellus hair, slight under‑eye unevenness. Plastic‑smooth skin is the single loudest AI tell.
Wardrobe kit. 3–5 named outfits with specific details (buttons, seams, jewelry). Reuse the kit; never let the model improvise clothing mid‑series.
B

Frame checks

every clip, before it renders into the batch
One light source, one story. Key light comes from a single stated direction per scene; shadows on face, neck, and background must all agree with it.
Catchlights match. The reflection in both eyes sits at the same clock position and matches the stated light source. Dead or mismatched eyes read as fake instantly.
Reflections obey the room. Glasses, jewelry, phone screens, and windows reflect the environment you claimed — not a studio void.
Micro‑expression pass. Blinks, slight facial asymmetry, small mouth movements between words. Symmetrical, frozen faces are the second‑loudest tell after skin.
Hands and teeth count. Five fingers, plausible knuckles, consistent teeth across smiles. Still the classic per‑frame failure points, even in 2026‑era generators.
Continuity within the clip. Same earrings, same shirt wrinkles, same hair part at second 1 and second 8.
C

Batch QA

after each batch, before anything posts
Line‑up test. Put one frame from every clip side by side against the master reference set. Same person in all of them, at a glance?
Mark check. Are both permanent marks present, in position, in every clip? A migrated mole means the clip regenerates.
Kill, don’t patch. A drifted clip gets regenerated from the master references, never “fixed” downstream — edits on top of drift read as uncanny.
Log the seeds. Whatever settings, prompts, or seeds produced on‑model results — write them down. Consistency is a filing habit as much as a craft skill.
D

Disclosure

non‑negotiable, every platform
Label the character as AI. Platforms require it. Fanvue puts every AI upload through a three‑moderator review and bans undisclosed AI on its public discovery and profile surfaces (per its published policy, as of 2026‑07‑21).
Disclosure is not the reach killer. Disclosed AI creators already account for roughly 15% of Fanvue’s revenue (Forbes, Jan 2026). The checklist above is about craft quality — not about passing the character off as human.

18 checks. Phases A and C are where consistency is actually won; phase B is where realism is won; phase D is what keeps the account alive.
